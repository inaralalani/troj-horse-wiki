Experimental Workflow


The following protocols are used in our experimental workflow. Click these buttons to access detailed instructions for each one.


Trojan horse's primary aim is to create a rapid antibody test. The antibody test we hope to create is a lateral flow immunoassay as shown below.

Figure 1: A designed lateral flow immunoassay test. Reference: https://www.frontiersin.org/files/Articles/602659/fbioe-08-602659-HTML/image_m/fbioe-08-602659-g004.jpg

Stage 1: Synthesis of recombinant genetic material

During Stage 1 the team will develop two different plasmid constructs as we hope to develop a composite test using two varying biomarkers. The first biomarker we hope to use is PNAG and the second biomarker we hope to use is DNABII; both of these biomarkers are antibodies. The final plasmid constructs of these biomarkers can be found under the Design tab. Figure 2: Plasmid Construct containing PNAG antibody gene sequence (Left) and DNABII antibody gene sequence (Right).

Stage 2: Insertion of recombinant genetic material

Once the plasmids have been created they must be inserted into our chosen chassis. The team picked E.coli as our chassis due to its rapid growth rate, cost efficiency, and simplicity in lab experiments. The creation of chemically competent E.coli is the first step which is followed by bacterial transformation; see protocols above. Figure 3: Post-development of recombinant plasmid and chemical transformation into chosen E.coli chassis.

Stage 3: Growth of Recombinant E.coli Cells

Now the recombinant E.coli cells have been obtained and need to be differentiated from the non-recombinant cells. The chosen antibiotic resistance gene Kanamycin will be critical for this process. The antibiotic gene on the plasmid is now present in only recombinant E.coli. Inoculation of the transformed E.coli on an agar plate containing the antibiotic will allow for the growth of only the recombinant cells. Figure 4: Distinction of transformed E.coli cells from non-transformed E.coli cells via antibiotic resistance gene.

Figure 5: Post-isolated transformed E.coli cell to express chosen protein; PNAG or DNABII antibodies

Stage 4: Protein extraction + Purification

Once the recombinant E.coli cells are isolated they need to begin protein expression. Protein expression can be done with an inducible promoter which allows for the expression of protein under specific conditions. A His-tag is added to the plasmid construct in order for protein purification can occur using the His-tag. Figure 6: Expressed proteins must be purified before use. His-Tag and Nickel affinity column protein purification can be used. See protocols above.

Stage 5: Measure Purified Protein

The final stage of this experiment involves measuring the protein we have extracted and purified. This is crucial as it serves as a proof of concept to show the protein synthesized and expressed is the protein we hoped to create in these previous experiments. This can be done in various ways; SDS Page, and Agarose Gel Electrophoresis. Figure 7: Protein purification is quantified by several methods; one of which is SDS as shown above.
